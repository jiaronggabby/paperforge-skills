# Paper Polish

Revise academic prose as a careful coauthor. Preserve technical meaning and the
document format unless the user asks for structural changes.

## Workflow

1. Identify the text's role.
   - Abstract, introduction, method, results, discussion, limitation, rebuttal,
     cover letter, thesis section, or grant section.
   - Preserve discipline conventions and required headings.

2. Align claims with evidence.
   - Separate descriptive results from statistical or causal claims.
   - Do not imply support from tests, baselines, datasets, or validation that are
     not present.
   - Keep limitations plain but not apologetic.

3. Remove generated-writing traces.
   - Cut generic openings such as "in recent years" and "it is important to note"
     unless they add information.
   - Replace vague praise with specific mechanisms, numbers, or constraints.
   - Avoid repeated "not only...but also", rule-of-three cadence, and stacked
     hedging such as "may potentially suggest".
   - Prefer ordinary verbs: shows, reduces, preserves, improves, matches, drops.
   - Vary sentence rhythm and avoid checklist-like paragraphs.

4. Preserve submission safety.
   - Expand acronyms at first use.
   - Mention every main figure/table naturally at least once.
   - Keep citations accurate and source-backed.
   - Do not insert workflow notes, prompt residue, or internal commentary into
     the manuscript.

## Output standard

The revised text should read like a human coauthor tightened it by hand:
natural, precise, confident about real evidence, and restrained where evidence
is incomplete.
