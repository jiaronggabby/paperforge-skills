# Bilingual Anti-AI Writing Skill

Use this skill when text sounds like it was assembled from generic AI advice: too balanced, too defensive, too smooth, too full of labels, or too eager to summarize itself. The goal is not to make writing casual. The goal is to make it read like a real researcher or professional wrote it with judgment.

## Core Principle

Keep the evidence and technical claims. Remove the scaffolding.

Good revision should make the argument feel more specific, more grounded, and more authored. Do not flatten the text into short plain sentences, and do not weaken novelty just to sound modest.

Use red-flag phrases as diagnostic clues, not banned words. A term is acceptable when it names a real method, construct, or contribution; it becomes AI-like when it substitutes for the argument. For example, "mechanism" is fine when the paragraph explains the mechanism, but weak when it merely says a project will "build a mechanism" without showing what changes, why, and how it will be tested.

Preserve:

- Necessary professional terms, dataset names, model names, statistical terms, and discipline-specific concepts.
- Clear novelty and grant-writing ambition.
- Evidence, numbers, citations, preliminary results, and explicit methodological scope.

Reduce:

- Summary voice before the reader has seen the problem.
- Excessive labels such as "path", "framework", "system", "closed loop", or "landscape" when they do not add content.
- Punctuation that imitates structure: stacked colons, quotation marks, semicolons, and formula-like lists.
- Defensive contrast that repeats "not X but Y" before the scientific tension is clear.

## 5-Point Diagnostic Rubric

Score each dimension from 1 to 5. A strong passage usually reaches 21/25 or above. When revising, improve the lowest-scoring dimension first instead of replacing every suspicious phrase.

| Dimension | 1 - AI-heavy | 3 - usable but stiff | 5 - human and field-ready |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Argument Flow | Announces a framework before the problem is felt | The logic is clear but segmented | The problem emerges naturally, then evidence sharpens it |
| Evidence Use | Uses vague claims and generic importance language | Gives evidence, but it feels inserted | Numbers, citations, or examples carry the claim |
| Sentence Rhythm | Repeats the same pattern and length | Mostly readable, with some template turns | Mixes long explanatory sentences with short judgment sentences |
| Terminology | Uses abstract labels without context | Terms are correct but over-repeated | Terms appear where needed and are explained through the argument |
| Voice and Judgment | Over-defensive, over-balanced, or promotional | Reasonable but impersonal | Shows clear authorial judgment without exaggeration |

### How to Judge a Phrase

Before deleting a phrase, ask four questions:

1. Does it point to a concrete object, operation, result, or source?
2. Would the sentence lose technical precision if it were removed?
3. Is the phrase doing argumentative work, or only announcing importance?
4. Does the same structural move appear in nearby paragraphs?

Keep the phrase when the answer to the first two questions is yes. Revise it when it mostly announces importance, repeats structure, or delays the evidence.

## Four-Pass Workflow

1. **Argument pass**: Find the main claim of the paragraph. If the paragraph starts by naming a framework or contribution before the problem is felt, move the problem, failure case, or observation forward.
2. **Evidence pass**: Ask what real evidence, result, citation, example, or preliminary finding supports the claim. Put the evidence before broad interpretation whenever possible.
3. **Mechanism pass**: Replace label-heavy summary with causal explanation. Show what changes, under what condition, and how the claim will be tested.
4. **Surface pass**: Read aloud for rhythm and punctuation. Remove filler transitions, stacked punctuation, repeated contrast frames, and paragraph endings that merely say the work is important.
5. **Ambition guard**: Recheck that novelty, technical depth, confidence, and professional tone were not weakened. The output should sound less synthetic, not less capable.

## Red Flags

Treat this section as a checklist for diagnosis. Do not mechanically delete every listed word.

### Chinese

Watch for these patterns:

- Over-signposting: "围绕上述主线", "形成路径", "推进框架", "纳入统一框架", "构建闭环"
- Defensive contrast: repeated "不是...而是...", "并非...而是...", "不仅...还..."
- Review-response tone: "与同类工作相比", "可检验命题", "边界场景", "压力测试", "入模条件"
- Excessive punctuation: too many colons, semicolons, quotation marks, and numbered formulas without textual connection
- Mechanical ending: "为...提供参考", "具有重要意义", "奠定基础", repeated at every section end

Natural replacements:

- Let the problem appear before the framework.
- Use "这一现象提示...", "申请人前期结果显示...", "因此需要解释..." when the logic truly follows from evidence.
- Prefer concrete verbs: "比较", "检验", "追溯", "定位", "修正", "验证".
- Keep necessary elegant Chinese constructions, but do not stack them.
- Replace broad endings with concrete outputs, such as an identifiable model, validation design, mechanism explanation, dataset, or decision criterion.

### English

Watch for these patterns:

- Inflated claims: "crucial", "pivotal", "robust framework", "paradigm shift", "serves as"
- Generic transitions: "Furthermore", "Additionally", "It is important to note that"
- Negative parallelism: "not merely X but Y", repeated "not only... but also..."
- Abstract nouns piled together: "landscape", "interplay", "synergy", "holistic", "comprehensive"
- Ending every paragraph with a broad future impact sentence

Natural replacements:

- Put the result before the interpretation.
- Use simple verbs when possible: "shows", "tests", "uses", "compares", "reduces", "fails".
- Let one sentence do one job.
- Keep field terms, but attach them to data, methods, or mechanisms.
- In cover letters, keep confidence but avoid generic praise. Name the fit between the paper and the journal/special issue through topic, audience, method, or contribution.

## Cross-Language Style Rules

Use these rules for Chinese proposals, English papers, and English cover letters.

1. **Problem before label**: Let the tension, limitation, or observation appear before naming the proposed framework, route, or contribution.
2. **Evidence before elevation**: Put data, preliminary results, citations, or concrete examples before claims about significance.
3. **Causality before slogan**: Replace "形成路径 / build a framework" with the causal steps the project actually performs.
4. **Precision before smoothness**: A slightly technical sentence is better than a smooth generic one.
5. **Variation without decoration**: Vary sentence length and paragraph endings, but do not add literary flourishes that distract from the science.
6. **Confidence without hype**: Keep innovation and novelty, but ground them in a demonstrable gap, result, or method.
7. **Contrast only when needed**: Use "不是...而是..." or "not X but Y" only when it resolves a real misconception. Repeated contrast sounds defensive.
8. **Punctuation must earn its place**: A colon should introduce a real explanation, a semicolon should connect two balanced clauses, and quotation marks should mark a term that truly needs marking.

## Academic Grant Pattern

For proposals, use this order:

1. Real-world or scientific tension.
2. What current methods can already do.
3. What they still fail to explain.
4. Applicant's preliminary evidence.
5. The sharper research question.
6. The planned method and validation.

Avoid starting with a large abstract framework. In Chinese grant writing, a strong paragraph often reads like:

> 现有方法已经能够..., 但在...条件下仍会...。申请人前期结果显示..., 这说明问题不在于..., 而在于...。因此，本项目拟...

Use the contrast only when it clarifies the science. Do not repeat this shape paragraph after paragraph.

For Chinese grant polishing, check three additional risks:

- **申报书总结腔**: Section endings repeatedly say "形成路径", "构建体系", "提供支撑", or "奠定基础" without naming the deliverable.
- **答辩腔提前进入正文**: The paragraph sounds like it is defending against reviewers rather than developing the scientific problem.
- **创新被稀释**: Anti-AI revision deletes too much ambition. Restore the strongest novelty claim, then attach it to evidence or validation.

## Manuscript Pattern

For papers, use this order:

1. Observation or gap.
2. Precise limitation in prior work.
3. Method idea.
4. Validation design.
5. Measured result.
6. Claim with scope.

Avoid turning every paragraph into "background - gap - contribution" if the reader already knows the context.

## Cover Letter Pattern

For English cover letters, use this order:

1. Manuscript title and article type.
2. One-sentence problem or field need.
3. The specific contribution and evidence base.
4. Why the journal's audience is the right audience.
5. Compliance statements if required.

Avoid generic sentences such as "We believe this manuscript will be of broad interest." Replace them with a concrete fit: what readers of the journal study, what method/result they can use, or what debate the paper informs.

## Revision Moves

### Replace AI scaffolding

Before:

> 本项目将构建一个系统化框架，形成从数据处理到模型验证的完整路径。

After:

> 项目先比较真实样本和合成样本的响应差异，再检验这些差异是否会转化为高水位残差。

Before:

> This work provides a robust and comprehensive framework for enhancing prediction performance.

After:

> The method reduces error only when the generated samples preserve the response structure needed by the predictor.

### Replace generic importance

Before:

> 该问题具有重要理论意义和实际应用价值。

After:

> 如果合成样本改变了风压响应或上尾分布，模型平均误差可能下降，但台风影响期的高水位仍会被低估。

Before:

> This is a critical challenge in the current landscape of machine learning.

After:

> The same augmentation can help a weak model and harm a stronger predictor, which means sample realism alone is not enough.

### Keep confidence without sounding promotional

Before:

> 本项目具有突出的创新性和广阔前景。

After:

> 申请人的前期结果已经给出清楚证据：直接增强在 PatchTST 上使 RMSE 从 0.584 升至 0.588，而结构化残差将 RMSE 降至 0.570。

## Final Pass Checklist

- Does each paragraph have one main job?
- Does the strongest evidence appear before the broad interpretation?
- Are there unnecessary labels such as "framework", "path", "system", or "mechanism"?
- Are technical terms introduced once and then used consistently?
- Did the revision remove AI traces without lowering the project's ambition?
- For Chinese, are commas and semicolons doing real syntactic work?
- For English, are transitions needed, or are they just smoothing filler?
- If the paragraph ends with "provide a reference/foundation", can it end with a concrete output instead?
- Has the revision preserved necessary technical terms and innovation?
- If a phrase was removed, was it replaced with evidence, causality, or a cleaner sentence rather than just deletion?

## Output Style

When using this skill, return:

1. A short diagnosis of the main AI-like patterns, optionally with the 5-point score if reviewing rather than directly editing.
2. A revised version.
3. A brief note on what changed and what technical content, novelty, or evidence was preserved.

For long documents, revise section by section and keep a small change log. Do not rewrite unrelated content just to make the prose uniform.
